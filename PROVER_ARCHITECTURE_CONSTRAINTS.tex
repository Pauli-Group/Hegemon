\begin{abstract}
We audit Hegemon's Plonky3 STARK prover against its 60-second PoW block
time, identify seven architectural flaws (five active, two resolved), and
derive a batch-first pipeline that scales from 0.1 to 1000+ TPS without
mode switches. Measured end-to-end: batch-16 proves in 21.5\,s on
32-core Xeon, verifies in 28\,ms (after VK caching), and the prove-ahead
pipeline includes 16/16 transactions per block at 1.95 inclusion TPS.
\end{abstract}

\section{Hard Constraints}

\begin{enumerate}[label=C\arabic*.,leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Full PQ security:} 128-bit soundness, no reduced parameters.
  \item \textbf{Fail-closed validity:} invalid proofs rejected at import.
  \item \textbf{Block-time bound:} all proving + verification $\le 60$\,s
        (\texttt{PowTargetBlockTime = 60\_000}).
  \item \textbf{Hardware reality:} heavy compute on \texttt{hegemon-prover}
        (32 cores, 256\,GB), not the laptop.
  \item \textbf{Acceptance gate:} sustained $\ge 10$~TPS for 30 minutes.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Architecture Audit}

Every claim below is derived from code constants and measurements, not
analogy. Sources: \texttt{aggregation/src/lib.rs} (1535 lines),
\texttt{circuits/batch/}, \texttt{circuits/block/},
\texttt{transaction-core/src/p3\_air.rs}, \texttt{p3\_config.rs},
\texttt{dimensions.rs}.

\subsection{What works}

\paragraph{Poseidon2 over Goldilocks.}
Correct choice: $\sim$2.5\,cycles/mul with AVX2 (2--3$\times$ faster than
BN254), PQ-secure (no lattice/DL structure), and STARK-compatible
($2^{32}$-smooth subgroup for NTT).

\paragraph{Transaction AIR.}
Well-designed: 32-row cycles fit the 31-step Poseidon2 permutation cleanly.
Constraint degree 8 matches quotient chunking (8 chunks, no waste).
Preprocessed round constants computed once at compile time.

\paragraph{Batch circuit.}
The key correct primitive. \texttt{BatchTransactionProverP3} proves up to 16
transactions in one STARK by concatenating traces:
$N_{\mathrm{batch}}(k) = (k \cdot 8192).\texttt{next\_power\_of\_two}()$.
Domain sizes $\{2^{13}, \ldots, 2^{17}\}$ for $k \in \{1, \ldots, 16\}$.
Same AIR, deterministic preprocessed traces, no dynamic shapes.

\paragraph{Commitment circuit.}
\texttt{CommitmentBlockProverP3}: 60 columns, Poseidon2 sponge over statement
hashes + nullifier permutation. Small trace ($2^{11}$ rows for 60\,tx),
fast ($<$0.5\,s), sound.

\paragraph{FRI parameters.}
Production: $\log_2 b = 4$, $q = 32 \Rightarrow \varepsilon = 2^{-128}$.
Debug: $\log_2 b = 3$, $q = 8 \Rightarrow 2^{-24}$. Feature-flag gating clean.

\subsection{Flaws}

\subsubsection{Flaw 1: Recursion is wrong for this throughput regime}

The aggregation circuit re-verifies $n$ inner STARK proofs inside an outer
STARK. Each inner proof contributes $\sim$63{,}000 field elements as symbolic
witnesses, and 64{,}192 S-box evaluations must be re-expressed as algebraic
constraints. Native STARK verification costs $<$10\,ms; re-verification
inside a recursion circuit costs seconds---a $>$50$\times$ overhead.
At $\le$60\,TPS, the succinctness gain (1 proof vs.\ a handful) is not worth
this cost.

\subsubsection{Flaw 2: Thread-local cache with $O(n_{\max})$ key space}

Cache key \texttt{(tx\_count, pub\_inputs\_len, log\_blowup, ProofShape)}
makes miss rate proportional to block occupancy variance. \texttt{thread\_local!}
storage prevents sharing across Rayon workers. Cold build: 100--465\,s per miss.

\subsubsection{Flaw 3: Batch scaling is linear, not sub-linear}

Batch-16 to batch-32 benchmark results:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lrrr@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Metric} & \textbf{batch-16} & \textbf{batch-32} &
  \textbf{Ratio} \\
\midrule
Prove time & 21.48~s & 42.84~s & $2.00\times$ \\
Verify time & 9.35~s & 18.59~s & $1.99\times$ \\
Throughput (prove$+$verify) & 0.52~tx/s & 0.52~tx/s & $1.00\times$ \\
Throughput (prove only) & 0.75~tx/s & 0.75~tx/s & $1.00\times$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Both proving and verification double when batch size doubles, yielding
constant per-transaction cost regardless of batch size. The theorised
sub-linear FRI/Merkle amortisation does not materialise because the trace
grows from $2^{17}$ to $2^{18}$ rows, and $O(N \log N)$ FRI/Merkle cost
actually grows \emph{slightly more} than $2\times$ due to the extra log
factor. Conclusion: \textbf{parallel batch-16 is optimal; increasing batch
size yields no throughput gain.}

\subsubsection{Flaw 4: Enormous outer trace in recursion}

For $n = 6$ inner proofs: outer trace $\sim 600{,}000 \to 2^{20}$ rows.
With blowup 16: LDE $= 50 \times 2^{24} \times 8 = 6.7$\,GB, far exceeding
any commodity L3 cache.

\subsubsection{Flaw 5: Unnecessary compression at low throughput}

With $\le$4 batch proofs per block, verification takes $<$40\,ms (invisible
at 60\,s block time). Compressing 4 proofs to 1 saves $\sim$30\,ms of
verifier time while costing $>$5\,s of prover time---a negative-value trade.
Recursion becomes justified only when batch-proof verification exceeds
$\sim$100\,ms ($\approx$60+ TPS).

\subsubsection{Flaw 6: VK recomputation on every verify call (resolved)}

Fixed: VK now cached by \texttt{(degree\_bits, log\_blowup)} in a
process-global \texttt{BTreeMap<RwLock>}. Prewarmed at node startup.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lrrl@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Before} & \textbf{After} & \textbf{Speedup} \\
\midrule
Batch-16 verify (bench) & 9.35~s & 28~ms & $332\times$ \\
Batch-32 verify (bench) & 18.59~s & 30~ms & $620\times$ \\
Node import verify (16~tx) & 5{,}751~ms & 137~ms & $42\times$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsubsection{Flaw 7: Prove-ahead balance\_tag hash poisoning (resolved)}

Fixed: \texttt{pending\_proofs} threaded through all 4 statement-hash call
sites. The coordinator's \texttt{BundleMatchKey} lookup now succeeds.

\paragraph{Root cause:} \texttt{statement\_hashes\_from\_extrinsics()} used
\texttt{pending\_proofs = None}, producing \texttt{balance\_tag = [0u8; 48]}
instead of the real tag from proof public inputs. Since
\texttt{compute\_transaction\_id()} feeds \texttt{balance\_tag} into SHA-384,
the lookup could never match.

\paragraph{Single-tx proof.}
For $M = 2^{17}$, $W = 132$: LDE 16M + Merkle 66M + constraints 23M +
FRI 4M = 109M cycles on 32 cores ($\sim$44\,ms). With 2--5$\times$
real-world overhead:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{T_{\mathrm{tx,single}} \approx 100\text{--}300\;\text{ms on 32 cores}
\;\textit{(estimate).}}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Batch-16 proof.}
$N = 2^{17}$, $M = 2^{21}$: LDE trace = 2.21\,GB (compute-bound, not
bandwidth-bound). Dominant cost: leaf hashing ($46 \times 10^6$ Poseidon2
perms), practical Merkle cost 400--600\,ms with column packing.
\begin{equation}
\boxed{T_{\mathrm{batch16}} \approx 1.5\text{--}3.0\;\text{s (estimate)}
\qquad T_{\mathrm{batch16}}^{\mathrm{measured}} = 21.5\;\text{s prove}
+ 28\;\text{ms verify.}}
\end{equation}
The 7--14$\times$ gap between estimate and measurement is consistent with
L3 cache pressure (2.2\,GB trace $\gg$ typical L3 capacity).

\subsection{Seamless scaling from 0.1 to $>$1000 TPS}

\section{Scaling Regimes}

The architecture must transition smoothly through throughput regimes without
mode switches. The pipeline is:
$\text{Batch}(k) \;\|\; \text{Merge}^{d}(a) \;\|\; \text{Commit}(n)$,
where recursion depth $d = 0$ at $\le$10 TPS and grows as $\lceil \log_8 B \rceil$
at higher loads.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}rrrllr@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{TPS} & \textbf{tx/blk} & \textbf{Batches} &
  \textbf{Prove HW} & \textbf{Recursion} & \textbf{Time (est.)} \\
\midrule
0.1 & 6 & 1 & 1 CPU (32c) & none & $<$1~s \\
1 & 60 & 4 & 1 CPU (32c) & none & $\sim$43~s \\
10 & 600 & 38 & 13 CPUs or 4 GPUs & none & $\sim$10~s (GPU) \\
100 & 6{,}000 & 375 & 8 GPUs & merge (depth 3) & $\sim$28~s \\
1{,}000 & 60{,}000 & 3{,}750 & 64 GPUs & merge (depth 4) & $\sim$2~s \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Rows at $\ge$100 TPS are projections from published GPU benchmarks
\cite{ingonyama2024,stwo2024}, not measurements on Hegemon's AIR.

\subsection{Regime 1 detail (1 TPS, current target)}

$\lceil 60/16 \rceil = 4$ batch-16 proofs. Two-wave parallelism on 32 cores
(16 cores each): $2 \times 21.5 = 43$\,s + 1.1\,s commit = 44\,s $ < 60$\,s.
Verification: $4 \times 17 + 17 \approx 85$\,ms. Proof payload: $\sim$825\,KB.

\subsection{Regime 2 detail (10 TPS)}

38 batch proofs. Single 32-core machine is infeasible ($817$\,s sequential).
GPU path: $38 \times 1$\,s / 4 GPUs $\approx 10$\,s. CPU cluster:
$\ge$13 machines marginally feasible.

\subsection{Regime 3 detail (100 TPS---recursion justified)}

375 batch proofs. Verification of 375 individual proofs takes 6.4\,s;
merge recursion reduces this to $\sim$30\,ms. Fixed-arity merge (arity 8)
over identical-shape batch proofs (same domain, same width, one cached
circuit)---unlike the current broken recursion over variable-count tx proofs.

GPU path: 23\,s batch + 5\,s merge level 1 + 3\,s level 2 + 0.5\,s root
$= 28$\,s pipelined critical path $< 60$\,s.
CPU-only is infeasible ($>$1000\,s).

\subsection{Regime 4 detail (1000+ TPS)}

3{,}750 batch proofs, tree depth 4. With 77 H100 GPUs: pipelined critical
path $\approx 2.2$\,s $\ll 60$\,s. Cost $\sim$\$2.3M for SWIFT-class volume.
At 10{,}000 TPS: $\sim$600 GPUs, tree depth 5. Linear hardware scaling.

\section{Target Architecture}

\subsection{Pipeline}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Partition:} $n$ tx $\to$ $\lceil n/16 \rceil$ batches.
  \item \textbf{Batch prove:} Independent, parallel, fixed AIR shape.
  \item \textbf{Merge (if batch count $> 8$):} Fixed-arity merge circuit.
        One cached shape, zero cold misses.
  \item \textbf{Commit:} Block commitment proof over all statement hashes.
  \item \textbf{Assemble:} Low load: $\lceil n/16 \rceil$ batch proofs +
        commit. High load: 1 root proof + commit.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Hardware: CPU batch + GPU recursion}

CPUs are efficient for batch proofs ($M \le 2^{21}$, streaming NTT).
Recursion proofs ($M \ge 2^{23}$, 3--7\,GB LDE) thrash the CPU cache
hierarchy; GPU bandwidth (3.35\,TB/s HBM3 on H100 vs.\ 200\,GB/s DDR5)
makes GPUs $10$--$20\times$ faster for merge proofs.

\section{Roadmap}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Phase 1 (now---prove-ahead functional):}
        Batch proving, VK caching, and prove-ahead pipelining verified
        end-to-end. 16/16 tx included per block. Measured: inclusion
        TPS $= 1.95$, e2e TPS $= 0.11$ (laptop-limited).
        \textbf{Immediate next:} move proving to \texttt{hegemon-prover}
        ($\to$ 21.5\,s build, fits in 60\,s block) and enable prover
        market fan-out (coordinator currently issues one work package
        at a time).

  \item \textbf{Phase 2 (2--4 weeks):}
        Multiple concurrent batch-16 provers on dedicated hardware.
        Build fixed-arity merge circuit (arity 8).
        Target: 5--50 TPS on CPU cluster.

  \item \textbf{Phase 3 (1--3 months):}
        GPU prover backend via Icicle \cite{ingonyama2024} or custom
        CUDA kernels. Same AIR/FRI; only backend replaced.
        Target: 100--1000+ TPS.

  \item \textbf{Phase 4 (6+ months):}
        Evaluate folding schemes (Nova \cite{nova2022}, HyperNova
        \cite{hypernova2023}) for the merge step. Only justified if
        merge becomes the bottleneck above $\sim$1000 TPS.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Summary}

Three correct primitives---the transaction AIR, the batch circuit, and the
block commitment circuit---exist but are composed with a recursion layer that
destroys performance. The fix is architectural: batch-first proving with no
recursion below 60 TPS, fixed-arity merge recursion above. Security is
preserved at every stage (same Poseidon2/Goldilocks/FRI, same 128-bit PQ
target). The only variable is how proofs are composed.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{0.1--1 TPS} & Batch-16 + prove-ahead. CPU only. Measured: 21.5\,s/batch. \\
\textbf{1--10 TPS} & Pipeline parallelism + CPU provers. No recursion. \\
\textbf{10--100 TPS} & GPU batch + fixed-arity merge. \\
\textbf{100--1000+ TPS} & GPU cluster. Tree depth $\sim \log_8 n$. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
